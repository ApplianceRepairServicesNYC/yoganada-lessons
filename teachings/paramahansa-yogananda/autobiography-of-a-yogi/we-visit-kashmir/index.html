Chapter 21

We Visit Kashmir

Right timing for right action

Chapter Summary

Overview

Eventually, when conditions were right and the guru's approval was given, the trip to Kashmir proved wonderful and fruitful. This chapter provides contrast to the previous one‚Äîthe same action yielded completely different results because the timing had changed.

What was unwise at one moment became appropriate at another. The developed soul learns to sense right timing; the undeveloped soul acts from impulse without regard for circumstance.

The students had learned their lesson from the previous failed attempt. This time they waited, heeding Sri Yukteswar's counsel, until conditions shifted. When the guru indicated the time was right, they made the same journey‚Äîbut with completely different results. The trip was smooth, enjoyable, and spiritually fruitful.

The contrast between the two journeys couldn't have been more stark. Same destination, same group, same intention‚Äîbut different timing, different outcome. What made the difference was not anything the students controlled directly, but their willingness to wait until circumstances were favorable and guidance was positive.

Core Teaching

What This Chapter Reveals

Timing is as important as action. The same action can have vastly different outcomes depending on when it is taken. Reading the flow of life is a spiritual skill.

Patience is not passivity. Waiting for right timing is different from procrastination or fear. Wise waiting is active discernment; procrastination is avoidance.

Circumstances change. What is wrong now may be right later. Flexibility and attunement to changing conditions serves better than rigid rules.

Modern culture valorizes speed and decisiveness, often treating hesitation as weakness. But wisdom traditions across cultures recognize the importance of timing. The Chinese concept of wu wei, the Jewish notion of "there is a time for everything," the agricultural wisdom of planting and harvesting in their seasons‚Äîall point to the same truth: when you act matters as much as what you do.

Sri Yukteswar could perceive the flow of circumstances in ways his students could not. His "no" at one time and "yes" at another wasn't arbitrary but reflected genuine perception of conditions. Learning to sense such conditions, even without the guru's level of perception, is a valuable spiritual skill.

This teaching challenges the ego's belief that willpower alone determines outcomes. We imagine that if we just push hard enough, we can force results. Sometimes we can‚Äîbut often at great cost, and sometimes not at all. The wise approach includes reading circumstances, waiting for support, and acting when conditions are favorable.

‚ú¶ The Art of Timing

Farmers know they can't force crops to grow faster by sheer effort. They prepare soil, plant at the right time, tend what needs tending, and wait for conditions to do their work. Spiritual timing follows similar principles: prepare what you can, act when conditions are right, and trust the process.

This isn't fatalism or passivity but active partnership with the flow of circumstances. The farmer isn't passive‚Äîbut they work with nature rather than against it.

Life Application

Applying This Today

Before acting on significant decisions, pause to assess timing. Is this the right moment? Are conditions supportive? Is there genuine inner green light, or are you forcing due to impatience?

Learn to distinguish between wise waiting and mere procrastination. One serves growth; the other avoids it.

This requires developing sensitivity to timing that goes beyond purely rational analysis. While you can assess some conditions logically, intuition and inner guidance often perceive what analysis cannot. The "gut feeling" that says "not yet" deserves attention, as does the sense that "now is the time."

The distinction between wise waiting and procrastination is crucial. They can look similar from outside but feel completely different inside. Wise waiting has a quality of alertness, readiness, active attention to conditions. Procrastination has a quality of avoidance, fear, excuses. Learn to recognize which you're doing.

Some situations genuinely require immediate action‚Äîemergencies don't wait for perfect timing. But many significant decisions can benefit from waiting until conditions are more favorable. The impatience that drives premature action is often ego rather than wisdom.

Conversely, when conditions are right, hesitation becomes the problem. If you've waited, prepared, and circumstances are favorable, continued waiting may be fear masquerading as patience. The skill is knowing when to wait and when to act‚Äîa skill that develops through practice and honest self-observation.

‚ú¶ Reading Conditions

How do you assess if conditions are favorable? Some factors can be analyzed: Is the external situation supportive? Are resources in place? Are potential obstacles addressed? But other factors require intuition: Does this feel like the right time? Is there inner resistance or inner permission? What does the "still small voice" say?

Developing this sensitivity requires practice. Pay attention to timing in your daily life. Notice when actions flow easily and when they encounter unexpected resistance. Over time, patterns emerge that help you read the flow more accurately.

Your Assignment

Practice Exercise

‚ú¶ Practice

Consider a significant decision currently before you. Rather than immediately acting, sit quietly and assess timing. Does this feel like the right moment? What conditions would need to be in place for the action to succeed? What is the quality of your inner guidance about timing?

Week One: Identify three significant actions you're considering. For each, assess timing: What conditions would make this action more likely to succeed? Are those conditions currently present? What does your intuition say about timing?

Week Two: Practice distinguishing wise waiting from procrastination. When you're not acting, honestly examine why. Is this alert readiness or fearful avoidance? What tells you the difference?

Week Three: Take a small action that you've been waiting on, where conditions now seem favorable. Notice how acting at the right time feels different from forcing action at the wrong time.

Week Four: Reflect on timing in your life. When have you acted at the right time? When have you forced action too early or waited too long? What patterns do you notice? What would help you read timing more accurately?

Going Deeper

Harmony with the Flow

The ultimate teaching of these paired chapters‚Äîthe failed trip and the successful one‚Äîis about harmony with the flow of life. When we work with the flow, things tend to go smoothly; when we work against it, we encounter friction and difficulty. The flow isn't arbitrary‚Äîit reflects real conditions that our ordinary awareness may not perceive.

Learning to sense this flow develops over time. It requires quieting the mind enough to perceive subtle signals, trusting intuition enough to act on it, and having the humility to recognize that your ordinary perspective is limited. The guru can see the flow clearly; students learn to sense it gradually.

This doesn't mean never facing difficulty or always waiting for easy conditions. Some paths are genuinely difficult regardless of timing. But even difficult paths go better when timing is right. The teaching is not to avoid all challenge but to move with rather than against the underlying current.

In Taoism, this is called wu wei‚Äînot-doing, or effortless action. It doesn't mean doing nothing but acting in harmony with the flow of circumstances. When you move with the flow, less effort produces greater results; when you move against it, great effort produces little. The wise person reads the flow and moves accordingly.
